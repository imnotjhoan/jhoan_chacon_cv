Project Details

This project represented one of the most complex challenges of my studies, covering everything from state-of-the-art research to the final implementation of a 3 GDL SCARA RRP robot. The development integrated mechanical design, power and control electronics, and DC motor control to achieve precise positioning and trajectory tracking. Using an iterative engineering approach, it was necessary to develop kinematic models, digital twins, and robust programming in C++. Its capabilities include:

  • RRP Kinematics: Combination of movement in the plane with vertical actuation.
  • Advanced Control: Since DC motors were used as actuators, it was necessary to implement the knowledge acquired in digital control.
  • Motion Planning: Trajectory simulation, workspace, point-to-point movement, and continuous trajectory.

CAD Design Process

Ideation Stage

The process began with a technical research phase, analyzing various configurations of SCARA robots with RRP architecture (two revolute joints and one prismatic joint). Based on this analysis, I developed a series of preliminary sketches and iterative CAD models. The main objective was to validate the spatial feasibility of the initial ideas, ensuring that each proposal complied with the dimensional limits and performance within the defined workspace.

Modeling and Refinement Stage

Based on the references researched, the first CAD models went through a series of revisions. This stage focused on refining the design, where we transformed complex geometries into a more efficient and simpler structure. The result was a design that avoids complete dependence on 3D printing and integrates MDF and acrylic, achieving a modular system that is easy and quick to manufacture and repair.

Manufacturing Process

For the materialization of the manipulator, a hybrid manufacturing approach was chosen, selecting the most suitable process for each component according to its structural and mechanical function. 3D printing was used for complex geometries, while for the bases and secondary housings (such as the electronics housing), I used laser cutting on acrylic and MDF, which resulted in shorter printing times, filament savings, and visibility of the belts and pulleys.

The design allows easy access to the motors and transmission systems, facilitating the adjustment of the belts/pulleys that can be seen in the assembly.

Bearings, bushings, pulleys, rotating plates, standard hardware, and motors with encoders were integrated, ensuring that the transition from the CAD to the physical prototype maintained the necessary tolerances for precision movement.
